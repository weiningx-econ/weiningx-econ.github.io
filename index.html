Publications

International Evidence on Vaccines and the Mortality to Infections Ratio in the Pre-Omicron Era

Abstract

Prior to the appearance of the Omicron variant, observations on countries like the UK that have accumulated a large fraction of inoculated individuals suggest that, although initially, vaccines have little effect on new infections, they strongly reduce the share of mortality out of a given pool of infections. This paper examines the extent to which this phenomenon is more general by testing the hypothesis that the ratio of lagged mortality to current infections is decreasing in the total number of vaccines per one hundred individuals in the pre-Omicron period, in a pooled time-series, cross-section sample with weekly observations for up to 208 countries. The main finding is that vaccines moderate the share of mortality from a given pool of lagged infections at sufficiently high levels of vaccination rates, which is essentially a favorable shift in the tradeoff between life preservation and economic performance. The practical lesson is that, in the presence of a sufficiently high share of inoculated individuals, governments can shade down containment measures, even as infections are still rampant, without significant adverse effects on mortality.

Gaps between official and excess Covid-19 mortality measures: The effects of institutional quality and vaccinations

Economic Modelling (2022), with J. Aizenman, A. Cukierman, Y. Jinjarak, and S. Nair-Desai

We evaluate quartile rankings of countries during the Covid-19 pandemic using both official (confirmed) and excess mortality data and study how institutional quality and vaccinations relate to differences between the two measures.

Pandemic Shocks and Fiscal-Monetary Policies in the Eurozone: COVID-19 Dominance During January–June 2020

Oxford Economic Papers (2021), with R. Ahmed, J. Aizenman, Y. Jinjarak, and S. Nair-Desai

We compare the importance of market factors against that of COVID-19 dynamics and policy responses in explaining Eurozone sovereign spreads and document “COVID-19 dominance” during the early pandemic period.

Accounting for Global COVID-19 Diffusion Patterns, January–April 2020

Economics of Disasters and Climate Change (2021), with R. Ahmed, J. Aizenman, Y. Jinjarak, and S. Nair-Desai

We document cross-country associations between COVID-19 mortality, policy interventions, and their interactions with institutional and demographic characteristics, highlighting conditions under which stricter policies correlate with lower future mortality growth.

Working Papers

Demystifying Trade Patterns In A Fragmenting World

IMF Working Paper (2025), with T. Schulze

We distinguish trade reallocation from trade rerouting using granular trade/FDI data and input–output linkages, with a focus on six Asian “connector” countries.

Gulf Cooperation Council Diversification: The Role of Foreign Investments and Sovereign Wealth Funds

IMF Working Paper (2025), with Y. Korniyenko

Using novel deal-level data, we study how cross-border investments (including by SWFs) relate to GCC growth and non-hydrocarbon diversification.

Market Power in the Middle East

IMF Working Paper (2025), with Y. Korniyenko and A. Tohamy

We document patterns of market power among listed firms in the Middle East and assess how policy changes (including VAT reforms in some Gulf states) relate to markups.

Dissecting Medium-Term Growth Prospects for Asia

IMF Working Paper (2024), with N. Che, F. Diez, and A. Oeking

We combine growth accounting, machine-learning estimation, and a pattern-matching forecasting algorithm (dynamic time warping) to study Asia-Pacific’s medium-term growth prospects.

Political Fragility: Coups d’État and Their Drivers  [Datasets and results]

IMF Working Paper (2024), with A. Cebotari, E. Chueca-Montuenga, Y. Diallo, Y. Ma, R. Turk, and H. Zavarce

We study coups d’état as a symptom of political fragility, combining event studies and machine learning to identify stressors, nonlinear interactions, and out-of-sample predictive performance.

U.S. Monetary Policy Spillovers to Middle East and Central Asia: Shocks, Fundamentals, and Propagations

IMF Working Paper (2024), with G. Ugazio

We decompose U.S. interest rate changes into monetary policy and information shocks and show these shocks have opposite spillovers to ME & CA economies, with heterogeneity linked to fundamentals.

International Reserves, Crisis Risk, and Risk Tolerance

SSRN Working Paper (submitted), with Y. Feng and X. Tong

We propose a two-stage framework that embeds an early-warning problem into a policy-making problem and analyze welfare tradeoffs between missed crises and false alarms.
